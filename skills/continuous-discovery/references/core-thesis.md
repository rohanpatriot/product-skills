# Core Thesis

The central argument of continuous discovery and the prerequisite mindsets required to practice it.

## Central Argument

Product teams must make decisions every day about what to build. Most teams make these decisions based on opinions, stakeholder requests, or the loudest voice in the room. Continuous discovery replaces opinion-driven decisions with evidence-informed decisions through a sustained practice of customer engagement.

**The definition of continuous discovery:**

> At minimum, weekly touchpoints with customers, by the team building the product, where they conduct small research activities, in pursuit of a desired outcome.

Every element of this definition matters:

- **Weekly** — Not quarterly research sprints. Not annual surveys. Weekly contact sustains learning and prevents the team from drifting away from customer reality.
- **By the team building the product** — Not delegated to a research department. The product trio (product manager, designer, engineer) must have direct contact with customers so that insights inform decisions in real time.
- **Small research activities** — Not months-long studies. Short interviews, quick prototype tests, one-question surveys. Small activities fit into the rhythm of delivery.
- **In pursuit of a desired outcome** — Not open-ended exploration. Discovery is focused by a clear outcome the team is trying to drive. The outcome provides direction and constraints.

## Six Prerequisite Mindsets

Continuous discovery requires more than process — it requires specific ways of thinking. Teams that adopt the practices without the mindsets will execute them mechanically and miss the point.

### 1. Outcome-Oriented

**The mindset:** Define success by outcomes, not outputs. Ask "What behavior change are we trying to drive?" before asking "What should we build?"

**What it replaces:** The feature-factory mindset where success means shipping what was on the roadmap.

**How to cultivate it:** Frame every initiative as a hypothesis — "We believe that [action] will result in [outcome] because [rationale]." Measure results, not just releases.

### 2. Customer-Centric

**The mindset:** Ground every decision in customer reality, not internal assumptions. Regularly engage with real customers. Let their needs, pain points, and desires drive the opportunity space.

**What it replaces:** The stakeholder-centric mindset where the loudest internal voice determines priorities.

**How to cultivate it:** Talk to customers every week. Make interview insights visible to the whole team. Challenge internal assumptions with customer evidence.

### 3. Collaborative

**The mindset:** The product trio (product manager, designer, engineer) makes discovery decisions together. No handoffs, no silos, no single decision-maker.

**What it replaces:** The sequential mindset where PM writes requirements, design creates mockups, and engineering implements.

**How to cultivate it:** Include all three roles in interviews, synthesis, and ideation. Make decisions as a group. Rotate who leads different activities.

### 4. Visual

**The mindset:** Externalize thinking into visual artifacts — Opportunity Solution Trees, experience maps, story maps, assumption maps. Visual tools make complexity manageable and shared understanding possible.

**What it replaces:** The document-heavy mindset where decisions live in long written specs that nobody reads the same way.

**How to cultivate it:** Draw before you write. Use trees and maps instead of lists and documents. Post visual artifacts where the team can see and update them.

### 5. Experimental

**The mindset:** Treat every decision as a hypothesis to be tested. Design small, fast experiments to reduce risk before committing resources. Expect to be wrong and design for learning.

**What it replaces:** The certainty mindset where teams commit fully to solutions based on confidence in their analysis.

**How to cultivate it:** Before building, ask "What would have to be true for this to work?" Test those assumptions cheaply. Celebrate fast learning, not just fast shipping.

### 6. Continuous

**The mindset:** Discovery is an ongoing practice, not a project with a start and end date. Build habits and rituals that sustain discovery alongside delivery every week.

**What it replaces:** The phase-gate mindset where research happens in a discovery phase and then stops.

**How to cultivate it:** Schedule recurring interviews. Build synthesis into your weekly cadence. Maintain living artifacts (Opportunity Solution Trees, experience maps) that evolve as you learn.
